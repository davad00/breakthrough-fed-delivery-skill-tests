Three Paths

The Stones of Practice

枯山水

Karesansui

Dry Landscape Meditation

In the dry garden, raked sand becomes ocean and stones become islands. The practitioner sits before emptiness and finds it full. Stillness is not the absence of movement — it is the presence of absolute attention. At Oasis, the karesansui practice strips away all ornament until only the essential remains: breath, awareness, the grain of sand beneath the rake.

茶庭

Chaniwa

Tea Garden Contemplation

The tea garden is a passage from the outer world to the inner. Each stepping stone is a decision. Each low gate requires a bow. The ritual of tea is not about the tea — it is about the deliberate attention brought to each gesture, each turn of the wrist, each moment of pouring. Oasis teaches the way of tea as the way of presence: nothing is ordinary when you attend completely.

築山

Tsukiyama

Hill Garden Movement

The hill garden is the garden of flow — waterfalls, streams, paths that wind and return. Movement here is not exercise; it is meditation in motion. The practitioner walks the circuit and discovers that the path changes with each circuit, not because the garden has changed, but because the walker has. At Oasis, tsukiyama practice integrates walking meditation with the landscape itself.
